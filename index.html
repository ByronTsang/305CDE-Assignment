Why People Take Part in Risk-Taking Behavior

Given that risk-taking behavior is potentially dangerous, some people wonder why anyone would take part in such conduct. On one hand, such behavior puts those who engage in it in harm's way, but, on the other, it gives participants the chance to experience an outcome they perceive as positive.

Who's at Risk for Risk-Taking Behavior?

Both male and female risk-takers share the same personality traits, such as impulsive sensation-seeking and sociability. Genetics play a role in risk-taking behavior as well. Identical twins separated at birth tend to engage in risk-taking behaviors at high rates. There's a gender imbalance in the people most likely to take part in risk-taking behaviors.

Examples of Risk-Taking Behavior

Risk-taking behavior includes having sex with strangers, often with no protection against sexually transmitted diseases or unplanned pregnancies. Risk-takers also enjoy gambling, typically losing more than they can handle. These individuals may also take part in extreme sports or recreational activities.

Prevention

United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime

Prevent risk taking behavior through sports

Aims to promote sports and related activities to prevent crime and to effectively build resilience of at-risk youth.

Strengthening the life skills of youth is a key objective in order to minimize risk factors and maximize protective factors related to crime, violence and drug use.

By enhancing knowledge of the consequences of crime and substance abuse and developing life skills, the initiative seeks to positively influence behaviour and attitudes of at-risk youth and prevent anti-social and risky behaviour.
